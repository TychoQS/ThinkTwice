Heuristics and cognitive biases
1.1 Scarcity principle Definition: People perceive products or opportunities as more valuable when they are limited in availability.
Example: “Only 3 items left in stock.”

1.2 Loss aversion Definition: People experience losses more strongly than gains of the same value.
Example: “Don’t miss this deal.”

1.3 Anchoring effect Definition: The first number or piece of information people see strongly influences later judgments.
Example: A product first shown as 199€ and then discounted to 79€.

1.4 Framing effect Definition: The way information is presented influences how people interpret it, even if the facts are identical.
Example: “90% fat-free” vs. “10% fat”.

1.5 Endowment effect Definition: People assign more value to things once they feel ownership over them.
Example: Free trials or product test periods.

1.6 Sunk cost fallacy Definition: People continue investing in something because they already spent time, effort, or money on it.
Example: Keeping a subscription because “I already paid for it.”

1.7 Availability heuristic Definition: People judge how likely something is based on how easily examples come to mind.
Example: Memorable or dramatic advertising stories.

1.8 Bandwagon effect Definition: People adopt behaviors or beliefs because many other people are doing the same.
Example: Viral trends or popular products.

1.9 Authority bias Definition: People trust statements more when they come from perceived experts or authority figures.
Example: “Recommended by doctors.”

Social influence strategies
2.1 Social proof Definition: People look at the behavior of others to decide what is correct or desirable.
Example: “Best seller” or “5000 customers bought this today.”

Pricing psychology
3.1 Charm pricing Definition: Prices are set just below a round number so they appear cheaper.
Example: 9.99 instead of 10.

3.2 Bundle pricing Definition: Multiple products are sold together as a package to make the overall offer appear more valuable.
Example: Fast food meal deals.

3.3 Partitioned pricing Definition: A price is split into smaller parts to make the base price appear lower.
Example: 29€ + 3€ shipping instead of 32€.

3.4 Decoy effect Definition: A deliberately less attractive option is added to make another option appear more appealing.
Example:
Basic – 8€ Standard – 12€ Premium – 13€
The Standard option appears like the best deal.

Dark patterns
4.1 Urgency / time pressure (is in there twice) Definition: Artificial time pressure is used to push faster decisions.
Example: “Offer ends in 10 minutes.”

4.2 Confirmshaming Definition: The interface shames users when they decline an offer.
Example: “No thanks, I don’t want to save money.”

4.3 Misdirection Definition: The interface design directs attention away from important information.
Example: A large “buy now” button while the cancel or decline option is small or hidden.


Questions:

Goal is to find out about:
- Motivation
- Practical Use
- Logic
- Financial situation

General questionair:
1. How would buying this affect your financial priorities this month?
2. What triggered your desire to buy this product?
3. If this product were double the price and not immediately available, would you still want it?
4. Describe a specific situation in the next 7 days where you will use this product.
5. What do you currently use instead, and why is it not sufficient?

1. Impulsive Shopper (emotion-driven)
1. What triggered your desire to buy this right now? (boredom, stress, social media, reward, other: ______)
2. What are you feeling right now that makes this purchase appealing?
3. If you had to wait 48 hours before buying, would you still want it? (Yes / No / Not sure)
4. Describe one specific situation in the next 7 days where you will use this.
5. How do you think you’ll feel 7 days after buying this?

2. Deal Hunter (discount-driven)
1. Would you still want this product if it were full price? (Yes / No / Not sure)
2. Did you plan to buy this before you saw the deal? (Yes / No)
3. How much money are you actually saving vs. spending? (open or guided input)
4. What problem does this product solve for you right now?
5. How many times would you realistically have needed this product in the past 7 days?

3. Functional Buyer (rationalizing needs)
1. What specific task can you not accomplish right now without this product?
2. What are you currently using instead, and why is it not sufficient?
3. How often will you realistically use this in the next 30 days?
4. What happens if you don’t buy this today?
5. Is this the simplest solution to your problem? (Yes / No / Not sure)

4. Budget-Constrained (financial pressure)
1. How does this purchase fit into your budget this month? (No impact / Slight impact / Significant impact)
2. What would you need to give up financially if you buy this?
3. Is this a need or a want for you right now? (Need / Want / Not sure)
4. How would you feel if an unexpected expense came up after buying this?
5. Will buying this move you closer to or further from your financial goals? (Closer / No impact / Further away)
